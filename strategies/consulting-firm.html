Consulting Firm

Students operate as professional consultants who diagnose a client's business problem and deliver an evidence-based, implementation-ready recommendation.

ConsultingCase AnalysisStrategyProfessional Communication

Quick Scan

Attribute Value
Time 45 minutes to several class periods
Prep Moderate to High
Movement Low to Moderate
Collaboration Very High
Technology Optional
Best Group Size 3–5 students

Best Uses

  • Case-study analysis
  • Strategic recommendations
  • Marketing plans
  • Financial problem solving
  • Entrepreneurship
  • Operations decisions
  • AP Business
  • Capstone projects

Why I Use It

The Consulting Firm simulation places students in the role of outside advisers hired to solve a real or fictional business problem. Students must understand the client's situation, identify the decision that matters most, use evidence, compare alternatives, and deliver a recommendation that the client could realistically implement.

The structure makes business analysis more authentic because students are not simply answering teacher questions. They are producing professional work for an audience with needs, constraints, and expectations.

Learning Outcomes

Students will be able to:

  • define a client's central business problem,
  • separate symptoms from root causes,
  • identify relevant qualitative and quantitative evidence,
  • evaluate realistic alternatives,
  • consider stakeholder effects,
  • develop an implementation-ready recommendation,
  • communicate findings professionally, and
  • respond to client questions and objections.

Choosing the Client Challenge

A strong consulting challenge includes:

  • a specific organization,
  • a clear decision or performance problem,
  • realistic constraints,
  • enough evidence for analysis,
  • multiple defensible options,
  • stakeholder consequences, and
  • a deliverable that fits the available class time.

Possible client problems:

  • declining sales,
  • weak brand awareness,
  • rising costs,
  • employee turnover,
  • poor customer satisfaction,
  • expansion decisions,
  • pricing problems,
  • capacity constraints,
  • cash-flow concerns,
  • event attendance,
  • sponsorship strategy,
  • product-launch decisions, or
  • international growth.

Materials

  • Client brief
  • Consulting team organizer
  • Evidence tracker
  • Data tables or exhibits
  • Decision matrix
  • Stakeholder map
  • Recommendation template
  • Presentation rubric
  • Client feedback form
  • Individual reflection
  • Optional slide template

Room Setup

Seat students in consulting teams of three to five. Each team needs access to the client brief, shared evidence, and one workspace for developing its recommendation.

For presentations, create a client meeting area where consultants can present and answer questions.

Running the Strategy

Phase 1: Client Briefing

  1. Introduce the client and business context.
  2. Clarify the decision the client must make.
  3. Explain the deliverable, audience, timeline, and evaluation criteria.
  4. Allow students to ask clarification questions.

Phase 2: Independent Review

  1. Students read and annotate the client materials.
  2. Each student writes a one-sentence problem statement.
  3. Students identify the three most important pieces of evidence.
  4. Students record questions or missing information.

Phase 3: Team Diagnosis

  1. Compare individual problem statements.
  2. Agree on the central decision.
  3. Separate symptoms from root causes.
  4. Identify constraints and opportunities.
  5. List assumptions that require testing.

Phase 4: Alternative Development

  1. Generate at least three possible responses.
  2. Establish decision criteria.
  3. Evaluate the alternatives.
  4. Identify likely stakeholder effects.
  5. Select the strongest recommendation.

Phase 5: Implementation Planning

The team should identify:

  • the first action,
  • responsible department or person,
  • timeline,
  • required resources,
  • expected cost,
  • success measures,
  • major risks, and
  • contingency actions.

Phase 6: Client Deliverable

Teams prepare one or more of the following:

  • executive summary,
  • consulting memo,
  • slide presentation,
  • dashboard,
  • financial analysis,
  • marketing plan,
  • implementation roadmap, or
  • oral client briefing.

Phase 7: Client Meeting

  1. Consultants present the recommendation.
  2. The client asks structured questions.
  3. Teams defend assumptions and evidence.
  4. The client provides feedback.
  5. Teams revise one part of the recommendation.

Consulting Team Roles

Engagement Manager

Keeps the project aligned with the client's problem, timeline, and required deliverables.

Research and Evidence Lead

Finds, organizes, and cites relevant evidence.

Financial Analyst

Completes calculations and evaluates costs, revenue, profitability, cash flow, or financial risk.

Strategy Analyst

Develops alternatives, decision criteria, and the final strategic recommendation.

Stakeholder and Implementation Lead

Evaluates stakeholder effects and develops the implementation plan.

Presentation Lead

Organizes the final deliverable and coordinates the client meeting.

For smaller teams, combine roles. Every student should still be prepared to answer client questions.

Teacher Language

  • What decision has the client actually hired you to help make?
  • Is that the problem, or is it a symptom of a deeper problem?
  • Which evidence is most relevant to the client's decision?
  • What assumption are you making?
  • What alternative did you reject, and why?
  • How does this recommendation create value?
  • What will implementation require?
  • Which stakeholder may resist this recommendation?
  • What could cause the recommendation to fail?
  • What should the client do during the first 30 days?
  • What evidence would cause your team to change its advice?

Timing Options

45-Minute Consulting Sprint

  • Client briefing: 5 minutes
  • Independent review: 7 minutes
  • Team diagnosis: 8 minutes
  • Alternatives and decision: 10 minutes
  • Executive recommendation: 10 minutes
  • Reflection: 5 minutes

90-Minute Block

  • Client briefing and questions: 10 minutes
  • Independent analysis: 10 minutes
  • Diagnosis and evidence: 15 minutes
  • Alternatives and decision matrix: 15 minutes
  • Implementation planning: 15 minutes
  • Client presentations: 20 minutes
  • Reflection: 5 minutes

Three-Class Consulting Project

Day 1

  • Client briefing
  • Research
  • Diagnosis
  • Evidence analysis

Day 2

  • Alternatives
  • Financial analysis
  • Recommendation
  • Implementation planning

Day 3

  • Client presentations
  • Questions
  • Feedback
  • Revision
  • Reflection

Multiweek Consulting Project

Students may conduct:

  • primary research,
  • customer surveys,
  • interviews,
  • competitor analysis,
  • financial modeling,
  • prototype development,
  • campaign design, or
  • formal client presentations.

Course Examples

Business Foundations

A neighborhood retailer has experienced declining customer traffic. Consulting teams diagnose possible causes and recommend a realistic 90-day response.

Marketing

A local restaurant wants to attract more customers ages 16–24. Teams develop a target-market analysis, positioning recommendation, promotional plan, budget, and success measures.

Personal Finance

A fictional client is struggling to balance debt repayment, emergency savings, insurance, and future goals. Teams create a prioritized financial action plan.

Entrepreneurship

A startup must decide whether to expand, change pricing, seek outside funding, or focus on profitability. Teams analyze the tradeoffs and recommend a path.

Economics

A manufacturer faces inflation, rising interest rates, and weaker demand. Teams recommend how the business should adjust pricing, production, staffing, and investment.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

A minor-league team wants to increase attendance and sponsorship revenue. Teams recommend a fan-engagement and partnership strategy within a fixed budget.

AP Business

Teams act as consulting firms responding to a complex case. Require analysis across business functions, relevant calculations, stakeholder effects, limitations, alternatives, implementation, and a justified recommendation.

Client Question Bank

Use questions such as:

  • What evidence most strongly supports your recommendation?
  • Which assumption creates the greatest risk?
  • Why is this better than the strongest alternative?
  • How much will this cost?
  • When should results become visible?
  • How will we measure success?
  • What should happen if the plan underperforms?
  • Which department owns implementation?
  • How will employees or customers respond?
  • What information do you still need?
  • What part of your recommendation is most uncertain?
  • What would make you advise us differently?

Freshman Supports

  • Use a shorter client brief.
  • Highlight essential evidence.
  • Limit teams to two alternatives.
  • Provide a partially completed decision matrix.
  • Use three simplified consulting roles.
  • Give students a recommendation template.
  • Model the difference between a symptom and a root cause.
  • Provide sentence starters.
  • Grade reasoning more heavily than presentation design.

Helpful sentence starters:

  • The client must decide whether...
  • The main problem appears to be...
  • One important piece of evidence is...
  • This evidence suggests...
  • We considered...
  • We recommend...
  • The client should first...
  • Success should be measured by...
  • The greatest risk is...

AP-Level Expectations

Require students to:

  • define the decision precisely,
  • synthesize evidence across business functions,
  • use quantitative analysis,
  • evaluate multiple alternatives,
  • distinguish evidence from assumptions,
  • consider short- and long-term effects,
  • analyze stakeholder consequences,
  • identify limitations,
  • develop a realistic implementation plan,
  • include measurable success criteria, and
  • explain what evidence could change the recommendation.

Common Problems and Fixes

Students Focus on Making Slides

Do not allow slide design until the diagnosis, evidence, alternatives, and recommendation are approved.

Teams Provide Generic Advice

Require a first action, owner, timeline, cost, success measure, and risk.

Students Treat Every Fact as Equally Important

Limit teams to the five pieces of evidence most important to the decision.

One Student Completes Most of the Work

Use role-specific checkpoints and an individual client-response reflection.

Recommendations Ignore Financial Reality

Require a cost estimate, financial tradeoff, or explanation of resource constraints.

Teams Avoid Disagreement

Require one student to defend the strongest rejected alternative.

Client Questions Become Random

Use the question bank and assign client roles such as owner, finance director, marketing director, operations manager, or employee representative.

Presentations Become Repetitive

Use simultaneous client panels, written executive summaries, consulting posters, or rotating client meetings.

Variations

Live Local Client

A community organization or business provides the challenge and feedback.

Internal Consulting

Teams advise a department within a larger company.

Crisis Consulting

Students receive a time-sensitive client problem and new information during the activity.

Specialist Consulting Firms

Each team focuses on finance, marketing, operations, human resources, or sustainability before sharing recommendations.

Competing Consulting Firms

Multiple teams respond to the same client brief and compete for the contract.

Consulting Carousel

Teams rotate among several short client problems.

Written Consulting Memo

Students submit a professional memo instead of presenting.

Recorded Client Briefing

Teams record a concise recommendation for asynchronous review.

Progressive Consulting Case

New client information is released over several rounds, requiring teams to revise their advice.

Assessment

Assess both the consulting product and the decision-making process.

Professional Quality Indicators

A strong consulting deliverable is:

  • client-centered,
  • evidence-based,
  • financially realistic,
  • specific,
  • actionable,
  • logically organized,
  • visually clear,
  • honest about limitations, and
  • prepared for implementation.

Final Thoughts

Consulting Firm is one of the most transferable business-classroom structures because it combines analysis, collaboration, communication, and implementation. Students learn that professional advice must do more than sound persuasive. It must respond to the client's actual decision, use relevant evidence, acknowledge constraints, and explain what should happen next.

The strategy becomes especially powerful when teachers reuse the same consulting process across different courses and cases. Over time, students become more independent because they know how to move from an unfamiliar business situation to a defensible, implementation-ready recommendation.
