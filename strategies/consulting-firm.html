Consulting Firm

Students operate as professional consultants who diagnose a client problem and deliver an evidence-based, implementation-ready recommendation.

Case analysisStrategic recommendationsMarketing planningOperations decisions
Teacher quick actionsSee the classroom →How to run it →

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

A client has hired the class because sales are falling, costs are rising, and the leadership team cannot agree on what to do next. Student consulting teams receive the same evidence, but they may reach very different conclusions. By the end of the meeting, each team must defend one clear recommendation and explain what the client should do first.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example challenge: A neighborhood entertainment venue has strong customer reviews but declining attendance and weak weekday revenue.

Students enter with a professional role, a clear decision, incomplete information, and a deliverable that another person must be able to use. The structure creates purposeful tension without turning the class into unstructured role-play.

Why This Simulation Works

Students operate as professional consultants who diagnose a client problem and deliver an evidence-based, implementation-ready recommendation.

The simulation gives students a reason to use course knowledge. They are not completing isolated questions. They are preparing for an audience, managing constraints, making tradeoffs, and producing a decision that can be challenged.

The strongest version makes thinking visible. Students should leave behind notes, calculations, criteria, evidence, revisions, and an individual explanation of what they contributed.

Best Uses

  • Case analysis
  • Strategic recommendations
  • Marketing planning
  • Operations decisions
  • AP Business case work
  • Capstone projects

Learning Outcomes

  • apply business concepts to a realistic organizational problem
  • use evidence rather than preference
  • make and defend a decision under constraints
  • communicate in a professional role
  • respond to questions and revise thinking
  • explain individual contribution and learning

Teacher Preparation

  1. Select a challenge with a real decision, multiple defensible options, and clear constraints.
  2. Prepare a short public brief and any role-specific or confidential information.
  3. Define the required product, audience, time limit, and decision criteria.
  4. Decide which evidence students receive at the start and which evidence may be released later.
  5. Prepare a visible timer, checkpoint prompts, and an individual accountability task.

Room Setup

Arrange desks in team pods with one shared workspace per team. Create a separate decision area for presentations, interviews, negotiations, or executive questioning. Keep role packets face down until the briefing is complete.

Timing Options

45-Minute Sprint

Briefing 5, preparation 8, core simulation 17, decision or presentation 10, reflection 5.

90-Minute Block

Briefing 10, role preparation 12, analysis 20, simulation 25, questioning 15, revision and reflection 8.

Multi-Day Version

Day 1 evidence and planning, Day 2 simulation and decision, Day 3 revision, extension, and assessment.

Teacher Language

  • What decision has the client actually hired you to help make?
  • Is that the problem, or a symptom of a deeper problem?
  • Which evidence carries the most weight?
  • What assumption could weaken your recommendation?
  • What should happen during the first 30 days?

Course Examples

Marketing

A local restaurant wants to attract more customers ages 16 to 24 without losing its current audience.

Personal Finance

A fictional household needs a prioritized plan for debt, emergency savings, insurance, and long-term goals.

Entrepreneurship

A startup must choose between expansion, a pricing change, outside funding, or a narrower product focus.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

A minor-league team needs to improve weekday attendance and sponsorship value.

AP Business

Students diagnose a case problem, test assumptions, compare alternatives, and defend a recommendation using evidence.

Common Problems and Fixes

Teams jump to solutions

Require a one-sentence problem statement and three evidence points before brainstorming.

One student controls the work

Assign role-specific deliverables and require every student to answer one client question.

Recommendations stay vague

Require owner, timeline, cost, success measure, risk, and first action.

Presentations become decorative

Score reasoning and implementation more heavily than visual design.

Variations

Consulting Sprint

Use a one-page client brief and one-slide recommendation when you need a complete decision cycle in 45 minutes.

Live Client

Invite a local business or school organization to share a real challenge. Use this when authentic audience pressure will improve student effort.

Competing Firms

Give every team the same client and let the client select the strongest recommendation. Use this to emphasize differentiation and defensibility.

Specialist Firms

Assign each team a specialty such as marketing, finance, operations, or human resources. Use this when students need deeper functional expertise.

Progressive Evidence Release

Release new exhibits during the simulation. Use this to test adaptability and prevent teams from locking into an early answer.

Assessment

Score the quality of the decision, not the performance alone.

Individual accountability options: role memo, decision justification, response to one challenge question, peer contribution rating, or revision explanation.

Student Reflection

  • What evidence most influenced your team's decision?
  • What tradeoff was hardest to manage?
  • What did another role notice that you initially missed?
  • What would you revise after hearing questions or feedback?
  • What would a real organization need before implementing this decision?
