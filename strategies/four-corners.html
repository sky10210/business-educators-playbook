Four Corners

Make students commit to a position, defend it, and reconsider after hearing stronger evidence.

MovementDiscussionOpinionDecision Making
Teacher quick actionsJump to Quick Scan →How to run it →

Quick Scan

Attribute Value
Time 10–30 minutes
Prep Low
Movement High
Discussion High
Technology Optional

Best Uses

  • Ethical business decisions
  • Marketing debates
  • Investment choices
  • Entrepreneurship tradeoffs
  • AP Business case discussions

Why I Use It

Four Corners forces every student to make a decision and physically commit to it.

Students cannot hide behind a group answer. They choose a position, defend it with evidence, listen to opposing viewpoints, and decide whether to keep or change their position.

Before Class

Create one prompt with four reasonable choices.

Avoid prompts with an obvious correct answer.

Good examples include:

  • Best advertising strategy
  • Best investment option
  • Best ownership structure
  • Best response to a customer crisis

Materials

  • Four corner signs
  • Prompt slide
  • Timer
  • Reflection sheet (optional)

Room Setup

Label each corner clearly. Leave walking space and establish expectations before movement begins.

Teacher Tip

The quality of the discussion depends on the quality of the choices. Every option should be defendable.

Shared Resources

This strategy uses the shared Teacher Language Library, Universal Discussion Rubric, and Timing Templates included in /content/shared/.

These common resources keep every Cornerstone strategy consistent while allowing course-specific examples to remain unique.

Running the Strategy

  1. Present one business scenario with four defensible choices.
  2. Students silently choose a corner.
  3. Corner groups develop the strongest argument.
  4. Groups present their reasoning.
  5. Students may switch corners if persuaded.
  6. Debrief by discussing what evidence changed opinions.

Teacher Language

  • Commit to the option you believe is strongest.
  • Your goal is to defend your position with evidence.
  • If another group convinces you, changing your mind is a strength.

Course Examples

Marketing

Which promotional strategy creates the highest ROI?

Personal Finance

Which savings goal should come first?

Entrepreneurship

Which funding source best balances growth and control?

Economics

Which policy has the greatest long-term benefit?

AP Business

Which recommendation creates the most stakeholder value?

Common Problems

  • Students follow friends → require silent individual decisions first.
  • One corner has everyone → temporarily split the corner into two discussion circles.
  • Weak discussion → rewrite the prompt so all four choices are realistic.

Variations

  • Digital polling before movement.
  • Silent evidence round before discussion.
  • Case-study version.
  • Data-analysis version.

Assessment

Evaluate:

  • Evidence used
  • Quality of reasoning
  • Participation
  • Willingness to revise thinking

Works Best With

Think–Pair–Share → Four Corners → Gallery Walk → Exit Ticket

Final Thoughts

Four Corners creates visible decision-making. Students must commit, defend, listen, and reconsider - the same process used by effective business leaders.
