Decision Matrix

Students define criteria, assign importance, score alternatives with evidence, and test whether a recommendation remains strong when assumptions change.

Option comparisonPrioritizationQuantitative reasoningStrategic decisions

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

Four options look promising, but the class cannot choose all of them. Students must decide what matters most before they score anything. Their matrix may identify a winner, but the real learning comes when they explain the weights, challenge the evidence, and test how easily the result could change.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example prompt: A student-run business must choose the best product to launch from four ideas with different costs, demand, risks, and operational requirements.

The structure makes participation and reasoning visible. Students must do more than state an opinion. They respond, connect, challenge, organize, or revise in a way the teacher can observe and assess.

Why This Structure Works

Students define criteria, assign importance, score alternatives with evidence, and test whether a recommendation remains strong when assumptions change.

The protocol slows down instinctive answers and gives students a repeatable way to interact with evidence and one another. It works best when the prompt includes uncertainty, tradeoffs, competing interests, or several plausible answers.

Best Uses

  • Option comparison
  • Prioritization
  • Quantitative reasoning
  • Strategic decisions
  • Purchasing
  • Personal finance choices

Teacher Preparation

  1. Choose a question that requires judgment rather than recall.
  2. Prepare the evidence, text, data, or idea cards students will use.
  3. Define the discussion moves and final product.
  4. Set participation expectations before the activity begins.
  5. Plan a synthesis task so the protocol produces a conclusion, not just activity.

90-Minute Version

Teacher Language

  • What makes this criterion relevant to the decision?
  • Are two criteria rewarding the same thing?
  • What evidence supports that score?
  • Would a reasonable person choose a different weight?
  • What change would cause another option to win?

Common Problems and Fixes

Students score first and invent criteria later

Lock criteria and weights before revealing full option details.

Numbers create false certainty

Require evidence notes, sensitivity testing, and a narrative risk check.

Teams manipulate weights to force a favorite

Use individual weighting first and require justification for changes.

The highest score is automatically accepted

Require a feasibility, ethics, and threshold review before the recommendation.

Variations

Rapid Matrix

Use three choices and four criteria for a short decision cycle.

Stakeholder Matrix

Different groups weight criteria from different stakeholder perspectives, then compare results.

Blind Option Matrix

Hide brand names or identities until scoring is complete to reduce bias.

Sensitivity Challenge

Change one important weight or score and ask whether the decision remains stable.

Minimum-Threshold Matrix

Require options to meet nonnegotiable ethical, legal, financial, or operational thresholds.

Assessment

Assess the quality of thinking rather than counting comments alone.

  • What conclusion can you now support?
