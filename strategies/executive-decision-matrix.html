Executive Decision Matrix

Students act as an executive team that defines decision criteria, weighs competing options, tests assumptions, and recommends a defensible course of action.

Strategic choicesCapital allocationLocation decisionsVendor selection
Teacher quick actionsSee the classroom →How to run it →

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

The organization has four possible paths and enough money to choose only one. Every option has benefits, costs, risks, and stakeholder consequences. Students cannot rely on instinct alone. They must decide which criteria matter, assign weights, score the evidence, and explain where the matrix helps and where judgment is still required.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example challenge: A growing company must choose between opening a new location, improving its current facility, expanding online sales, or developing a new service.

Students enter with a professional role, a clear decision, incomplete information, and a deliverable that another person must be able to use. The structure creates purposeful tension without turning the class into unstructured role-play.

Why This Simulation Works

Students act as an executive team that defines decision criteria, weighs competing options, tests assumptions, and recommends a defensible course of action.

The simulation gives students a reason to use course knowledge. They are not completing isolated questions. They are preparing for an audience, managing constraints, making tradeoffs, and producing a decision that can be challenged.

The strongest version makes thinking visible. Students should leave behind notes, calculations, criteria, evidence, revisions, and an individual explanation of what they contributed.

Best Uses

  • Strategic choices
  • Capital allocation
  • Location decisions
  • Vendor selection
  • Ethics
  • Quantitative reasoning

Learning Outcomes

  • apply business concepts to a realistic organizational problem
  • use evidence rather than preference
  • make and defend a decision under constraints
  • communicate in a professional role
  • respond to questions and revise thinking
  • explain individual contribution and learning

Teacher Preparation

  1. Select a challenge with a real decision, multiple defensible options, and clear constraints.
  2. Prepare a short public brief and any role-specific or confidential information.
  3. Define the required product, audience, time limit, and decision criteria.
  4. Decide which evidence students receive at the start and which evidence may be released later.
  5. Prepare a visible timer, checkpoint prompts, and an individual accountability task.

Materials

Room Setup

Arrange desks in team pods with one shared workspace per team. Create a separate decision area for presentations, interviews, negotiations, or executive questioning. Keep role packets face down until the briefing is complete.

Timing Options

45-Minute Sprint

Briefing 5, preparation 8, core simulation 17, decision or presentation 10, reflection 5.

90-Minute Block

Briefing 10, role preparation 12, analysis 20, simulation 25, questioning 15, revision and reflection 8.

Multi-Day Version

Day 1 evidence and planning, Day 2 simulation and decision, Day 3 revision, extension, and assessment.

Teacher Language

  • What makes a criterion decision-relevant?
  • Are two criteria measuring the same thing?
  • What evidence supports this score?
  • How would the outcome change if the weights changed?
  • What important issue cannot be reduced to a number?

Course Examples

Marketing

Choose among four campaign concepts or target markets.

Personal Finance

Choose among housing, transportation, insurance, or college options using weighted criteria.

Entrepreneurship

Select a supplier, location, funding path, or customer segment.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Choose a host city, sponsor, venue, or promotional partnership.

AP Business

Students combine quantitative analysis, stakeholder effects, uncertainty, and strategic judgment.

Common Problems and Fixes

Students invent scores without evidence

Require a citation or assumption note beside every score.

The matrix creates false precision

Add a narrative risk check and sensitivity analysis.

Criteria overlap

Ask teams to test whether each criterion measures something distinct.

Weights are manipulated to force a preferred answer

Require weights before option scoring.

Variations

Rapid Matrix

Use three options and four criteria for a 25-minute decision.

Stakeholder Matrices

Different stakeholder teams build separate matrices and compare outcomes.

Blind Options

Label choices A, B, C, and D during scoring to reduce brand or popularity bias.

Sensitivity Challenge

Change one major weight after teams decide. Use this to test robustness.

Ethical Override

Include a minimum ethical or legal threshold that can disqualify a high-scoring option.

Assessment

Score the quality of the decision, not the performance alone.

Student Reflection

  • What evidence most influenced your team's decision?
  • What tradeoff was hardest to manage?
  • What did another role notice that you initially missed?
  • What would you revise after hearing questions or feedback?
  • What would a real organization need before implementing this decision?
