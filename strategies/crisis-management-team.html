Crisis Management Team

Students form a cross-functional response team that stabilizes a business crisis, communicates with stakeholders, and builds a recovery plan as new information arrives.

Risk managementPublic relationsOperationsEthics
Teacher quick actionsSee the classroom →How to run it →

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

At 9:05 a.m., the company learns that a product may be unsafe. At 9:12, a customer video begins spreading online. At 9:20, a reporter requests comment. Student teams must decide what to stop, what to say, who to contact, and how to protect people before the next update changes the situation again.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example challenge: A beverage company receives reports that several bottles may contain an undeclared allergen while social media posts are spreading rapidly.

Students enter with a professional role, a clear decision, incomplete information, and a deliverable that another person must be able to use. The structure creates purposeful tension without turning the class into unstructured role-play.

Why This Simulation Works

Students form a cross-functional response team that stabilizes a business crisis, communicates with stakeholders, and builds a recovery plan as new information arrives.

The simulation gives students a reason to use course knowledge. They are not completing isolated questions. They are preparing for an audience, managing constraints, making tradeoffs, and producing a decision that can be challenged.

The strongest version makes thinking visible. Students should leave behind notes, calculations, criteria, evidence, revisions, and an individual explanation of what they contributed.

Best Uses

  • Risk management
  • Public relations
  • Operations
  • Ethics
  • Leadership
  • Decision-making under pressure

Learning Outcomes

  • apply business concepts to a realistic organizational problem
  • use evidence rather than preference
  • make and defend a decision under constraints
  • communicate in a professional role
  • respond to questions and revise thinking
  • explain individual contribution and learning

Teacher Preparation

  1. Select a challenge with a real decision, multiple defensible options, and clear constraints.
  2. Prepare a short public brief and any role-specific or confidential information.
  3. Define the required product, audience, time limit, and decision criteria.
  4. Decide which evidence students receive at the start and which evidence may be released later.
  5. Prepare a visible timer, checkpoint prompts, and an individual accountability task.

Room Setup

Arrange desks in team pods with one shared workspace per team. Create a separate decision area for presentations, interviews, negotiations, or executive questioning. Keep role packets face down until the briefing is complete.

Timing Options

45-Minute Sprint

Briefing 5, preparation 8, core simulation 17, decision or presentation 10, reflection 5.

90-Minute Block

Briefing 10, role preparation 12, analysis 20, simulation 25, questioning 15, revision and reflection 8.

Multi-Day Version

Day 1 evidence and planning, Day 2 simulation and decision, Day 3 revision, extension, and assessment.

Teacher Language

  • What must happen in the next ten minutes?
  • Who could be harmed if the team waits?
  • What can you say with confidence right now?
  • Which action protects trust even if it costs money?
  • What evidence would change your response level?

Course Examples

Marketing

A campaign is criticized for using an offensive stereotype.

Personal Finance

A financial-services firm experiences a customer data breach.

Entrepreneurship

A startup's only supplier suddenly shuts down.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Severe weather threatens a sold-out outdoor event.

AP Business

Students balance stakeholder protection, continuity, reputation, cost, and ethical responsibility.

Common Problems and Fixes

Teams focus only on public relations

Require operational containment before the public statement.

Students try to hide uncertainty

Teach them to state what is known, unknown, being investigated, and happening next.

Every update causes panic

Require a decision log so teams can distinguish facts from assumptions.

The loudest student becomes crisis director

Use assigned roles and timed reporting rounds.

Variations

Single-Round Crisis

Use one crisis packet and one response plan for a 30-minute lesson.

Escalating Crisis

Release three or four updates that complicate the original response. Use this to assess adaptability.

Media Scramble

Add student reporters who request interviews and publish short updates. Use this to emphasize message discipline.

Ethics Fault Line

Create a legal option that is ethically weak. Use this to force discussion beyond compliance.

Recovery Quarter

Continue the scenario into a second class where students rebuild sales, employee trust, and operations.

Assessment

Score the quality of the decision, not the performance alone.

Individual accountability options: role memo, decision justification, response to one challenge question, peer contribution rating, or revision explanation.

Student Reflection

  • What evidence most influenced your team's decision?
  • What tradeoff was hardest to manage?
  • What did another role notice that you initially missed?
  • What would you revise after hearing questions or feedback?
  • What would a real organization need before implementing this decision?
