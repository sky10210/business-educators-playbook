Expert Groups

Small groups investigate one specialized business topic, create a high-quality teaching product, and serve as the class resource for questions, feedback, and application.

ResearchStudent expertiseCollaborative teachingReference creation
Teacher quick actionsSee the classroom →How to run it →

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

The class becomes a temporary consulting firm. Each group owns a specialty, develops real expertise, and must produce something useful enough that other students can rely on it.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example challenge: Groups become specialists in different business functions, then create concise reference tools and advise classmates during a larger case or project.

Students depend on one another for information, but the final learning still belongs to each individual. The structure combines collaboration, teaching, questioning, and synthesis.

Why This Strategy Works

Small groups investigate one specialized business topic, create a high-quality teaching product, and serve as the class resource for questions, feedback, and application.

Students process content more deeply when they must teach it, respond to questions, and connect their section to a larger business problem. Clear roles and an individual check prevent collaboration from becoming uneven group work.

Best Uses

  • Research
  • Student expertise
  • Collaborative teaching
  • Reference creation
  • Project support
  • Deep content

Teacher Preparation

  1. Choose content that divides into meaningful but connected sections.
  2. Balance the workload and difficulty across groups.
  3. Prepare evidence, sources, success criteria, and note structures.
  4. Plan how students will teach, question, and verify information.
  5. End with an individual product that requires the full learning target.

Teacher Language

  • What makes this group an expert rather than a summarizer?
  • Which sources or evidence are trustworthy?
  • How will the group make complex content usable?
  • What questions should classmates be able to ask this group?
  • How will every student show individual understanding?

Course Examples

Marketing

Groups specialize in segmentation, branding, pricing, promotion, research, or analytics and advise campaign teams.

Personal Finance

Groups specialize in banking, credit, insurance, taxes, investing, or consumer protection and build class reference guides.

Entrepreneurship

Groups become experts in customer discovery, business models, finance, legal issues, operations, or pitching.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Groups specialize in sponsorship, ticketing, media, fan engagement, event operations, or venue revenue.

AP Business

Groups become experts in framework sections, industries, stakeholders, evidence types, or strategic options within a case.

Common Problems and Fixes

Groups divide work without shared expertise

Require every member to pass a group knowledge check before presenting.

Resources become decorative

Use a quality checklist focused on accuracy, clarity, examples, and usefulness.

Research takes too long

Provide a curated source set, clear boundaries, and a fixed deliverable.

Classmates ignore expert groups

Build a later task that requires consultation with multiple expert teams.

Variations
