Product Launch Simulation

Students coordinate customer research, product design, pricing, distribution, promotion, operations, and risk to build and defend a complete launch plan.

Marketing mixCross-functional planningPricingEntrepreneurship
Teacher quick actionsSee the classroom →How to run it →

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

The launch date is fixed, the budget is limited, and every department wants something different. Marketing wants attention, finance wants margin, operations wants simplicity, and customers want value. Student launch teams must align every decision before executives approve the plan.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example challenge: A snack company is launching a high-protein product for high school and college athletes with a fixed first-quarter budget.

Students enter with a professional role, a clear decision, incomplete information, and a deliverable that another person must be able to use. The structure creates purposeful tension without turning the class into unstructured role-play.

Why This Simulation Works

Students coordinate customer research, product design, pricing, distribution, promotion, operations, and risk to build and defend a complete launch plan.

The simulation gives students a reason to use course knowledge. They are not completing isolated questions. They are preparing for an audience, managing constraints, making tradeoffs, and producing a decision that can be challenged.

The strongest version makes thinking visible. Students should leave behind notes, calculations, criteria, evidence, revisions, and an individual explanation of what they contributed.

Best Uses

  • Marketing mix
  • Cross-functional planning
  • Pricing
  • Entrepreneurship
  • Operations
  • Integrated campaigns

Learning Outcomes

  • apply business concepts to a realistic organizational problem
  • use evidence rather than preference
  • make and defend a decision under constraints
  • communicate in a professional role
  • respond to questions and revise thinking
  • explain individual contribution and learning

Teacher Preparation

  1. Select a challenge with a real decision, multiple defensible options, and clear constraints.
  2. Prepare a short public brief and any role-specific or confidential information.
  3. Define the required product, audience, time limit, and decision criteria.
  4. Decide which evidence students receive at the start and which evidence may be released later.
  5. Prepare a visible timer, checkpoint prompts, and an individual accountability task.

Room Setup

Arrange desks in team pods with one shared workspace per team. Create a separate decision area for presentations, interviews, negotiations, or executive questioning. Keep role packets face down until the briefing is complete.

Timing Options

45-Minute Sprint

Briefing 5, preparation 8, core simulation 17, decision or presentation 10, reflection 5.

90-Minute Block

Briefing 10, role preparation 12, analysis 20, simulation 25, questioning 15, revision and reflection 8.

Multi-Day Version

Day 1 evidence and planning, Day 2 simulation and decision, Day 3 revision, extension, and assessment.

Teacher Language

  • Does every decision serve the same target customer?
  • How does the price connect to cost, value, and competition?
  • Can operations actually deliver what marketing promises?
  • What must be true for the sales forecast to work?
  • What is the first metric you will watch after launch?

Course Examples

Marketing

Launch a new beverage, clothing line, subscription, or school event.

Personal Finance

Launch a student financial-wellness service with a sustainable fee model.

Entrepreneurship

Launch a minimum viable product and test market assumptions.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Launch a new theme night, fan membership, or esports event.

AP Business

Students integrate customer, value proposition, channels, revenue, resources, costs, partners, and risk.

Common Problems and Fixes

Students start with a logo

Require target customer and customer problem approval before creative work.

Pricing is a guess

Require cost, competitor, value, and break-even evidence.

Promotion is only social media

Require message, channel, timing, budget, and conversion goal.

Functions work separately

Use launch checkpoints where every decision must align across roles.

Variations

Launch Sprint

Use a compact brief and one-page launch canvas for a 60-minute block.

Relaunch

Give students an existing product with weak sales or reputation. Use this to emphasize diagnosis and repositioning.

Limited-Budget Launch

Reduce available funds and force teams to prioritize channels and features.

International Launch

Add cultural, regulatory, currency, and distribution constraints.

Progressive Market Update

Release competitor moves or customer feedback during planning. Use this to test adaptation.

Assessment

Score the quality of the decision, not the performance alone.

Individual accountability options: role memo, decision justification, response to one challenge question, peer contribution rating, or revision explanation.

Student Reflection

  • What evidence most influenced your team's decision?
  • What tradeoff was hardest to manage?
  • What did another role notice that you initially missed?
  • What would you revise after hearing questions or feedback?
  • What would a real organization need before implementing this decision?
