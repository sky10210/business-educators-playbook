Case Study Teams

Students operate as management teams to diagnose a case, evaluate evidence and alternatives, and defend an implementation-ready recommendation.

Attribute Value
Time 30–90 minutes
Prep Moderate

Collaboration Very High

Best Uses

  • AP Business case analysis

  • Financial interpretation
  • Stakeholder analysis
  • Strategic recommendations

Why I Use It

Case Study Teams give students a repeatable structure for making business decisions from incomplete information. Rather than dividing a case into disconnected questions, students work as a management team that must identify the central problem, interpret evidence, compare alternatives, and defend one recommendation.

The structure helps students move beyond summary. They learn that strong business analysis depends on selecting relevant evidence, recognizing tradeoffs, and connecting decisions across marketing, finance, operations, human resources, and strategy.

Before Class

Choose a case with:

  • a clear decision point,
  • enough evidence to support multiple interpretations,
  • realistic constraints,
  • stakeholder consequences,
  • quantitative or qualitative data, and
  • no single obvious answer.

Prepare a team organizer with the same analysis sequence students will use throughout the course.

Materials

  • Case study
  • Team analysis organizer
  • Evidence tracker
  • Decision matrix
  • Calculator or spreadsheet when needed
  • Recommendation template
  • Individual reflection
  • Optional role cards

Room Setup

Seat students in teams of three to five. Every student should be able to see the case, the evidence tracker, and the team's developing recommendation.

Running the Strategy

  1. Introduce the organization, context, and decision.
  2. Give students time to read and annotate independently.
  3. Ask each student to identify the central problem in one sentence.
  4. Have teams compare problem statements and agree on the most important decision.
  5. Assign or select team roles.
  6. Collect relevant evidence from the case.
  7. Separate facts, assumptions, and missing information.
  8. Identify realistic alternatives.
  9. Evaluate alternatives using agreed criteria.
  10. Select one recommendation.
  11. Explain implementation, risks, and stakeholder effects.
  12. Present, defend, or submit the decision.
  13. End with individual reflection or revision.

Core Analysis Sequence

1. Define the Decision

What must management decide now?

A strong decision statement is specific, actionable, and connected to the organization's goals.

2. Diagnose the Situation

What is causing the problem?

Students should distinguish:

  • symptoms,
  • root causes,
  • constraints,
  • opportunities, and
  • evidence gaps.

3. Identify Relevant Evidence

Which facts actually affect the decision?

Evidence may include:

  • financial data,
  • customer research,
  • employee information,
  • competitor behavior,
  • operational capacity,
  • market trends,
  • stakeholder concerns, and
  • organizational objectives.

4. Develop Alternatives

What realistic choices does management have?

Require at least two viable alternatives. “Do nothing” may be included only when students explain its likely consequences.

5. Establish Decision Criteria

How should the alternatives be judged?

Possible criteria:

  • cost,
  • revenue potential,
  • speed,
  • risk,
  • feasibility,
  • customer impact,
  • employee impact,
  • strategic fit,
  • ethics, and
  • long-term sustainability.

6. Select and Justify

Which alternative is strongest and why?

Students should compare alternatives rather than simply describe the preferred option.

7. Plan Implementation

What should happen next?

Include:

  • first action,
  • responsible person or department,
  • timeline,
  • resources,
  • success measure, and
  • contingency response.

Team Roles

Facilitator

Keeps the team focused on the decision and ensures all members contribute.

Evidence Manager

Locates, records, and cites case evidence.

Quantitative Analyst

Completes calculations and interprets financial or numerical data.

Stakeholder Analyst

Evaluates effects on customers, employees, owners, suppliers, and the community.

Skeptic

Challenges assumptions, tests the recommendation, and identifies risks.

Recorder or Presenter

Organizes the final analysis and communicates the team's decision.

For smaller groups, combine roles. For larger groups, assign both a recorder and presenter.

Teacher Language

  • State the decision before discussing solutions.
  • Which evidence makes that conclusion more likely?
  • Is that a fact from the case or an assumption?
  • What is the root cause rather than the visible symptom?
  • What alternative did you reject, and why?
  • Which stakeholder gains, and which stakeholder carries the cost?
  • What would make your recommendation fail?
  • What should management do first?
  • What evidence could change your decision?

Timing Options

30-Minute Case Sprint

  • Independent read: 5 minutes
  • Problem and evidence: 8 minutes
  • Alternatives and decision: 8 minutes
  • Recommendation: 6 minutes
  • Reflection: 3 minutes

55-Minute Class

  • Context and reading: 10 minutes
  • Diagnosis and evidence: 12 minutes
  • Alternatives and criteria: 10 minutes
  • Decision and implementation: 10 minutes
  • Presentations or comparison: 8 minutes
  • Reflection: 5 minutes

90-Minute Block

  • Introduction and independent annotation: 15 minutes
  • Team diagnosis: 15 minutes
  • Evidence and calculations: 15 minutes
  • Alternatives and decision matrix: 15 minutes
  • Recommendation and implementation: 15 minutes
  • Defense, feedback, and reflection: 15 minutes

Course Examples

Business Foundations

Students advise a local business experiencing declining sales. They identify possible causes, compare realistic responses, and recommend the first action management should take.

Marketing

Teams analyze a company facing weak brand awareness or poor campaign performance. They use customer, competitor, and promotional evidence to recommend a revised strategy.

Personal Finance

Students act as financial counselors for a fictional household. They evaluate income, spending, debt, insurance, savings, and goals before recommending a realistic plan.

Entrepreneurship

Teams advise a startup facing a growth, funding, pricing, staffing, or capacity decision. They must balance opportunity with cash needs and execution risk.

Economics

Students analyze how a business should respond to inflation, unemployment, interest-rate changes, regulation, tariffs, or shifting consumer demand.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Teams advise a team, venue, event, artist, or league facing weak attendance, sponsorship concerns, fan dissatisfaction, pricing problems, or brand controversy.

AP Business

Students use a full business case to integrate evidence from multiple syllabus areas. Require quantitative analysis, stakeholder impact, alternative evaluation, implementation, limitations, and a justified recommendation.

Freshman Supports

  • Shorten the case.
  • Highlight essential evidence.
  • Provide a vocabulary box.
  • Use three clear team roles.
  • Give students a partially completed organizer.
  • Limit the decision to two or three alternatives.
  • Model the difference between a fact and an assumption.
  • Provide sentence starters.
  • Grade the quality of reasoning more than the length of the response.

Helpful sentence starters:

  • The main decision is...
  • One important fact is...
  • This suggests that...
  • One possible cause is...
  • The strongest alternative is...
  • We rejected the other option because...
  • Our recommendation could fail if...
  • Management should first...

AP-Level Expectations

Require students to:

  • identify the key decision precisely,
  • integrate evidence across business functions,
  • complete relevant calculations,
  • distinguish correlation from causation,
  • evaluate short- and long-term effects,
  • compare realistic alternatives,
  • analyze stakeholders,
  • acknowledge limitations,
  • propose implementation,
  • include contingency planning, and
  • explain what additional evidence would strengthen or change the recommendation.

Common Problems and Fixes

Students Retell the Case

Limit summary to two or three sentences and require the remaining work to focus on diagnosis, alternatives, and decision making.

Teams Choose a Solution Too Early

Do not allow recommendations until teams have recorded evidence, alternatives, and decision criteria.

One Student Carries the Analysis

Use assigned roles, individual annotations, and a final individual reflection.

Students Use Opinion Instead of Evidence

Require evidence citations or line references on the organizer.

Recommendations Are Too General

Require a first action, owner, timeline, resource, and success measure.

Students Ignore Quantitative Data

Assign a quantitative analyst and require the team to explain what each calculation means for the decision.

Teams Agree Too Quickly

Use a skeptic role or require one member to defend the strongest rejected alternative.

Presentations Become Repetitive

Use comparison charts, simultaneous team posters, written executive summaries, or random selection of only a few teams to present.

Variations

Consulting Team

Students prepare a recommendation for a fictional client and deliver a concise consulting brief.

Board of Directors

Teams present to a board that questions the recommendation from owner, employee, customer, and community perspectives.

Crisis Team

Students respond to a time-sensitive event with limited information and must update the decision as new evidence appears.

Case Jigsaw

Different students analyze finance, marketing, operations, human resources, or stakeholders before rejoining the management team.

Silent Case Analysis

Students contribute written evidence and recommendations before discussion begins.

Competing Management Teams

Several teams analyze the same case and compare recommendations using one shared rubric.

Progressive Case

Release the case in stages. Teams must revise their diagnosis and recommendation as new information arrives.

Case Conference

Teams first meet privately, then send one representative to compare thinking with another team before making the final decision.

Assessment

Assess both team analysis and individual understanding.

Team Criteria

  • Accuracy of decision statement
  • Quality of diagnosis
  • Relevance of evidence
  • Quantitative reasoning
  • Alternative evaluation
  • Stakeholder analysis
  • Recommendation quality
  • Implementation
  • Risk and contingency planning
  • Communication

Individual Criteria

  • Annotation quality
  • Contribution to the team
  • Use of evidence
  • Reflection
  • Ability to defend or revise the decision

  • Fishbowl for observing case discussion
  • Shark Tank for recommendation defense
  • Exit Ticket for individual accountability

Final Thoughts

Case Study Teams provide the backbone for rigorous business instruction because they mirror how real organizations make decisions: people interpret incomplete evidence, disagree about priorities, compare options, and act despite uncertainty.

The most important design choice is consistency. When students repeatedly use the same analysis sequence - decision, diagnosis, evidence, alternatives, criteria, recommendation, implementation, and risk - they become more independent and more capable of transferring business reasoning to unfamiliar situations.
