Jigsaw 2.0

Students become specialists on one part of a larger topic, teach that expertise to a new group, and complete an individual synthesis task that requires connections across all sections.

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

Each student holds one piece of the complete business problem. No one can finish the final challenge alone, so every learner must understand one section well enough to teach it clearly and accurately.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example challenge: Students investigate separate parts of a company case, then regroup to build a complete recommendation using evidence from every expert section.

Students depend on one another for information, but the final learning still belongs to each individual. The structure combines collaboration, teaching, questioning, and synthesis.

Why This Strategy Works

Students become specialists on one part of a larger topic, teach that expertise to a new group, and complete an individual synthesis task that requires connections across all sections.

Students process content more deeply when they must teach it, respond to questions, and connect their section to a larger business problem. Clear roles and an individual check prevent collaboration from becoming uneven group work.

Best Uses

  • Complex content
  • Case analysis
  • Shared reading
  • Student teaching
  • Synthesis
  • Individual accountability

Teacher Preparation

  1. Choose content that divides into meaningful but connected sections.
  2. Balance the workload and difficulty across groups.
  3. Prepare evidence, sources, success criteria, and note structures.
  4. Plan how students will teach, question, and verify information.
  5. End with an individual product that requires the full learning target.

Teacher Language

  • What must every expert be able to explain?
  • What evidence will prevent inaccurate peer teaching?
  • How will listeners record and question each section?
  • What connects the expert sections into one larger idea?
  • How will you verify individual understanding at the end?

Course Examples

Marketing

Experts analyze target market, product, price, place, promotion, and metrics before teams recommend a campaign.

Personal Finance

Experts teach budgeting, credit, insurance, taxes, saving, and investing before students solve a financial plan.

Entrepreneurship

Experts examine customer, problem, value proposition, channels, costs, and revenue before teams revise a business model.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Experts study audience, sponsorship, ticketing, promotion, operations, and media before designing an event strategy.

AP Business

Experts analyze separate Business Model Canvas sections or case evidence categories before writing an integrated recommendation.

Common Problems and Fixes

Expert sections are uneven

Balance reading length, difficulty, and importance before assigning topics.

Students teach inaccurate information

Require expert checks, evidence notes, and a teacher verification point.

Listeners remain passive

Give every listener a required note frame and question responsibility.

Final understanding stays fragmented

End with synthesis and an individual task requiring connections across all sections.

Variations

Mini Jigsaw

Use four short sections in one class period.

Case Evidence Jigsaw

Assign stakeholder, financial, market, operational, or ethical evidence.

Visual Jigsaw

Experts analyze charts, advertisements, financial statements, or maps.

Two-Round Jigsaw

Students teach once, revise explanations, and teach a second audience.

Digital Jigsaw

Use shared documents, breakout rooms, and individual synthesis forms.
