Chalk Talk

Students build a visible web of ideas, questions, evidence, and connections around a business concept without requiring immediate oral participation.

BrainstormingConcept connectionsRoot-cause analysisPrior knowledge
Teacher quick actionsSee the classroom →How to run it →

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

A single business question sits in the center of a large page. Students begin adding causes, consequences, questions, examples, and counterpoints. Lines form between ideas. Clusters emerge. The class can literally see how its thinking is developing.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example prompt: Students map why a well-known retail brand may be losing relevance with younger customers.

The structure makes participation and reasoning visible. Students must do more than state an opinion. They respond, connect, challenge, organize, or revise in a way the teacher can observe and assess.

Why This Structure Works

Students build a visible web of ideas, questions, evidence, and connections around a business concept without requiring immediate oral participation.

The protocol slows down instinctive answers and gives students a repeatable way to interact with evidence and one another. It works best when the prompt includes uncertainty, tradeoffs, competing interests, or several plausible answers.

Best Uses

  • Brainstorming
  • Concept connections
  • Root-cause analysis
  • Prior knowledge
  • Quiet participation
  • Unit launch

Teacher Preparation

  1. Choose a question that requires judgment rather than recall.
  2. Prepare the evidence, text, data, or idea cards students will use.
  3. Define the discussion moves and final product.
  4. Set participation expectations before the activity begins.
  5. Plan a synthesis task so the protocol produces a conclusion, not just activity.

Timing Options

20-Minute Version

Prompt and think time 4, protocol 10, synthesis 4, individual close 2.

45-Minute Version

Preparation 8, first round 12, deepen or reorganize 12, synthesis 8, reflection 5.

90-Minute Version

Add source analysis, role preparation, a second round, revision, and formal assessment.

Teacher Language

  • Add a cause, consequence, example, question, or connection.
  • Draw a line and explain the relationship.
  • Which ideas belong together?
  • What is missing from this map?
  • What claim could the class make based on the pattern?

Common Problems and Fixes

The board becomes a list

Require lines, labels, questions, and responses to existing ideas.

Students add duplicates

Ask them to extend, qualify, or challenge rather than repeat.

The activity ends without synthesis

Require cluster names and a final written claim.

Handwriting or space becomes a barrier

Use sticky notes or a digital board with zoom and accessibility support.

Variations

Root-Cause Chalk Talk

Organize contributions into symptoms, causes, evidence, and consequences.

Stakeholder Chalk Talk

Give each stakeholder a separate color or region. Use this to expose conflicting interests.

Question-Only Round

Students may only add questions during the first pass. Use this to improve inquiry before solution generation.

Evidence Overlay

After brainstorming, give students exhibits and require evidence tags beside supported ideas.

Before-and-After Map

Repeat the same prompt at the end of a unit and compare how the network of thinking changed.

Assessment

Assess the quality of thinking rather than counting comments alone.

Student Reflection

  • Which idea or piece of evidence most influenced your thinking?
  • What connection or disagreement helped the group move forward?
  • What did you revise?
  • Whose perspective was missing or underdeveloped?
  • What conclusion can you now support?
