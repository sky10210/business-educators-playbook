Company Turnaround

Students take control of a struggling organization, diagnose the causes of decline, choose difficult priorities, and present a measurable recovery plan.

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

The company has six months of cash left. Sales are falling, employee morale is weak, and customers are leaving. Every possible response creates pain somewhere. Student turnaround teams must decide what to protect, what to cut, what to change, and how to prove that the business can recover before time runs out.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example challenge: A once-popular clothing retailer has declining store traffic, excess inventory, high rent, weak online sales, and an unclear brand.

Students enter with a professional role, a clear decision, incomplete information, and a deliverable that another person must be able to use. The structure creates purposeful tension without turning the class into unstructured role-play.

Why This Simulation Works

Students take control of a struggling organization, diagnose the causes of decline, choose difficult priorities, and present a measurable recovery plan.

The simulation gives students a reason to use course knowledge. They are not completing isolated questions. They are preparing for an audience, managing constraints, making tradeoffs, and producing a decision that can be challenged.

The strongest version makes thinking visible. Students should leave behind notes, calculations, criteria, evidence, revisions, and an individual explanation of what they contributed.

Best Uses

  • Turnaround strategy
  • Financial analysis
  • Change management
  • Operations
  • Marketing strategy
  • Leadership

Learning Outcomes

  • apply business concepts to a realistic organizational problem
  • use evidence rather than preference
  • make and defend a decision under constraints
  • communicate in a professional role
  • respond to questions and revise thinking
  • explain individual contribution and learning

Teacher Preparation

  1. Select a challenge with a real decision, multiple defensible options, and clear constraints.
  2. Prepare a short public brief and any role-specific or confidential information.
  3. Define the required product, audience, time limit, and decision criteria.
  4. Decide which evidence students receive at the start and which evidence may be released later.
  5. Prepare a visible timer, checkpoint prompts, and an individual accountability task.

Room Setup

Arrange desks in team pods with one shared workspace per team. Create a separate decision area for presentations, interviews, negotiations, or executive questioning. Keep role packets face down until the briefing is complete.

Timing Options

45-Minute Sprint

Briefing 5, preparation 8, core simulation 17, decision or presentation 10, reflection 5.

90-Minute Block

Briefing 10, role preparation 12, analysis 20, simulation 25, questioning 15, revision and reflection 8.

Multi-Day Version

Day 1 evidence and planning, Day 2 simulation and decision, Day 3 revision, extension, and assessment.

Teacher Language

  • What must be stabilized before growth is possible?
  • Which cost cut would damage the recovery?
  • What will the company stop doing?
  • Which customer should the company fight to keep?
  • What evidence after 30 days would show that the plan is working?

Course Examples

Marketing

Turn around a brand that has lost relevance with its target market.

Personal Finance

Create a household financial turnaround after an income loss.

Entrepreneurship

Rescue a startup with weak cash flow and poor product-market fit.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Recover a team, venue, or festival with falling attendance and sponsor dissatisfaction.

AP Business

Students integrate finance, customer value, resources, activities, stakeholders, change, and measurable hypotheses.

Common Problems and Fixes

Teams cut everything

Require identification of capabilities and customer value that must be protected.

Plans promise growth without stabilization

Require immediate cash, operations, and trust actions before expansion.

Students list many initiatives

Limit the plan to three major priorities and a stop-doing list.

Milestones are vague

Require baseline, target, owner, deadline, and trigger for adjustment.

Variations

90-Day Turnaround

Focus only on stabilization and the first quarter.

Private Equity Challenge

Give teams a required return and debt constraints. Use this for advanced finance.

Mission-Driven Turnaround

Use a nonprofit or social enterprise where service impact and financial survival must both be protected.

Leadership Change

Introduce a new chief executive with different priorities midway through planning.

Recovery Dashboard

Continue the simulation with monthly results and require teams to revise the plan.

Assessment

Score the quality of the decision, not the performance alone.

Student Reflection

  • What evidence most influenced your team's decision?
  • What tradeoff was hardest to manage?
  • What did another role notice that you initially missed?
  • What would you revise after hearing questions or feedback?
  • What would a real organization need before implementing this decision?
