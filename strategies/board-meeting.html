Boardroom Meeting

Students serve as executives and board members who evaluate a major proposal, question assumptions, manage competing interests, and vote on a final course of action.

GovernanceStakeholder analysisStrategic decisionsEthics
Teacher quick actionsSee the classroom →How to run it →

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

The executive team believes it has a winning plan. The board is not convinced. Finance sees risk, marketing sees opportunity, employees fear disruption, and investors want growth. Students enter the room with different responsibilities and must decide whether the organization should approve, revise, delay, or reject the proposal.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example challenge: A regional retailer is considering closing two stores and investing the savings in e-commerce and same-day delivery.

Students enter with a professional role, a clear decision, incomplete information, and a deliverable that another person must be able to use. The structure creates purposeful tension without turning the class into unstructured role-play.

Why This Simulation Works

Students serve as executives and board members who evaluate a major proposal, question assumptions, manage competing interests, and vote on a final course of action.

The simulation gives students a reason to use course knowledge. They are not completing isolated questions. They are preparing for an audience, managing constraints, making tradeoffs, and producing a decision that can be challenged.

The strongest version makes thinking visible. Students should leave behind notes, calculations, criteria, evidence, revisions, and an individual explanation of what they contributed.

Best Uses

  • Governance
  • Stakeholder analysis
  • Strategic decisions
  • Ethics
  • Corporate finance
  • Executive communication

Learning Outcomes

  • apply business concepts to a realistic organizational problem
  • use evidence rather than preference
  • make and defend a decision under constraints
  • communicate in a professional role
  • respond to questions and revise thinking
  • explain individual contribution and learning

Teacher Preparation

  1. Select a challenge with a real decision, multiple defensible options, and clear constraints.
  2. Prepare a short public brief and any role-specific or confidential information.
  3. Define the required product, audience, time limit, and decision criteria.
  4. Decide which evidence students receive at the start and which evidence may be released later.
  5. Prepare a visible timer, checkpoint prompts, and an individual accountability task.

Room Setup

Arrange desks in team pods with one shared workspace per team. Create a separate decision area for presentations, interviews, negotiations, or executive questioning. Keep role packets face down until the briefing is complete.

Timing Options

45-Minute Sprint

Briefing 5, preparation 8, core simulation 17, decision or presentation 10, reflection 5.

90-Minute Block

Briefing 10, role preparation 12, analysis 20, simulation 25, questioning 15, revision and reflection 8.

Multi-Day Version

Day 1 evidence and planning, Day 2 simulation and decision, Day 3 revision, extension, and assessment.

Teacher Language

  • What information would a responsible board need before approving this?
  • Which stakeholder carries risk without receiving the benefit?
  • What condition could be added to make the proposal safer?
  • Are you evaluating the idea or simply defending your role?
  • What will the board monitor after approval?

Course Examples

Marketing

Approve or reject a major rebrand that may alienate loyal customers.

Personal Finance

A nonprofit board decides how to use a limited emergency assistance fund.

Entrepreneurship

A startup board evaluates a founder proposal to accept outside investment in exchange for ownership.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

A venue board decides whether to sign a controversial naming-rights sponsor.

AP Business

Students evaluate strategic fit, stakeholder effects, risk, implementation, and governance.

Common Problems and Fixes

Students read speeches instead of deliberating

Limit opening statements and reserve most time for questions and response.

The vote feels predetermined

Require at least two amendments or conditions before the final vote.

Roles become stereotypes

Give each role both opportunities and concerns rather than a single fixed position.

Quiet students disappear

Require each board member to ask a question and explain a vote.

Variations

Emergency Board Meeting

Introduce a time-sensitive crisis and shorten preparation. Use this when you want fast judgment under pressure.

Committee Review

Send the proposal through finance, ethics, operations, and marketing committees before the full meeting. Use this for longer projects.

Shareholder Challenge

Allow shareholders to question the board after the vote. Use this to add accountability and public justification.

Split Board

Give two board factions different priorities. Use this to create negotiation and coalition-building.

Conditional Approval

Require the board to attach milestones, reporting requirements, or limits. Use this to teach oversight rather than simple yes-or-no voting.

Assessment

Score the quality of the decision, not the performance alone.

Student Reflection

  • What evidence most influenced your team's decision?
  • What tradeoff was hardest to manage?
  • What did another role notice that you initially missed?
  • What would you revise after hearing questions or feedback?
  • What would a real organization need before implementing this decision?
