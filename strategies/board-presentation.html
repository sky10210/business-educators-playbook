Board Presentation

Students present a strategic recommendation to a board, justify it with evidence, address financial and stakeholder consequences, and respond to governance-level questions.

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

The board has reviewed the pre-read. They do not need a summary of every page. They need to know what decision is being requested, why it is justified, what risks remain, and how leadership will measure success.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example challenge: Students ask a fictional board to approve a major expansion, new product line, or operational investment.

Students face an audience empowered to challenge assumptions and make a decision. The presentation becomes a professional recommendation that must withstand questioning.

Why This Strategy Works

Students present a strategic recommendation to a board, justify it with evidence, address financial and stakeholder consequences, and respond to governance-level questions.

Students must prioritize decision-relevant evidence, make assumptions visible, connect recommendations to financial and stakeholder consequences, and respond when the audience tests the logic.

Best Uses

  • Strategic recommendations
  • Governance decisions
  • Evidence synthesis
  • Financial justification
  • Risk analysis
  • Executive communication

Teacher Preparation

  1. Define the decision, audience, evidence set, and constraints.
  2. Clarify what students must quantify, estimate, or label as an assumption.
  3. Provide an audience role sheet with distinct decision lenses.
  4. Require a rehearsal or draft checkpoint before final delivery.
  5. Set expectations for questions, professional disagreement, and revision.

Timing Options

40-Minute Sprint

Brief 5, planning 15, rehearsal 6, presentation and questions 10, reflection 4.

55-Minute Lesson

Brief 6, analysis 18, preparation 10, presentation and questions 16, reflection 5.

90-Minute Block

Add financial modeling, audience preparation, longer questioning, and a formal revision memo.

Teacher Language

  • What exact approval are you requesting?
  • Which evidence makes this a board-level issue?
  • What is the financial consequence of doing nothing?
  • Which stakeholder could oppose this decision and why?
  • How will the board know the strategy is working?

Course Examples

Marketing

Request approval for a major campaign, rebrand, sponsorship, or market-entry strategy.

Personal Finance

Present a fictional nonprofit or household financial recovery plan to an advisory board.

Entrepreneurship

Ask a startup board to approve hiring, expansion, pivoting, or a new funding use.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Request approval for a venue renovation, pricing strategy, event launch, or partnership.

AP Business

Translate case evidence into a strategic decision with financial, operational, ethical, and stakeholder implications.

Common Problems and Fixes

Students give a class presentation instead of a board presentation

Require an explicit decision request, strategic rationale, risks, measures, and implementation accountability.

Financial claims are vague

Require assumptions, simple calculations, ranges, or clearly labeled unknowns.

The deck is overloaded

Use a concise board deck with supporting evidence moved to an appendix.

Questioning becomes superficial

Give board members assigned lenses such as finance, operations, customer, ethics, or risk.

Variations

Five-Slide Board Deck

Limit teams to decision, evidence, financial impact, risk, and implementation.

Board Pre-Read

Students submit a one-page memo before a shorter live question session.

Divided Board

Board members receive different priorities and must negotiate conditions for approval.

Conditional Approval

The board may approve, reject, or approve with required milestones.

Quarterly Update

Teams return later with simulated results and explain whether the strategy should continue.

Student Reflection

  • What was the most important decision your audience had to make?
  • Which evidence most strengthened your recommendation?
  • Which assumption or risk remained weakest?
  • Which audience question changed or refined your thinking?
  • What would you revise before the next decision meeting?
