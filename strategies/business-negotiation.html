Business Negotiation

Students prepare interests, priorities, alternatives, and tradeable terms before negotiating an agreement that creates value and protects essential needs.

NegotiationContractsPurchasingSales
Teacher quick actionsSee the classroom →How to run it →

Classroom Trailer

Picture the room

Two organizations need each other, but they do not want the same deal. One side values price, the other values timing. One needs publicity, the other needs control. Students receive confidential information, prepare their walk-away point, and enter a negotiation where the best agreement may depend on discovering what the other side values differently.

A Real Classroom Snapshot

Example challenge: A school event organizer negotiates a sponsorship agreement with a local company involving cash, promotion, exclusivity, tickets, and community visibility.

Students enter with a professional role, a clear decision, incomplete information, and a deliverable that another person must be able to use. The structure creates purposeful tension without turning the class into unstructured role-play.

Why This Simulation Works

Students prepare interests, priorities, alternatives, and tradeable terms before negotiating an agreement that creates value and protects essential needs.

The simulation gives students a reason to use course knowledge. They are not completing isolated questions. They are preparing for an audience, managing constraints, making tradeoffs, and producing a decision that can be challenged.

The strongest version makes thinking visible. Students should leave behind notes, calculations, criteria, evidence, revisions, and an individual explanation of what they contributed.

Best Uses

  • Negotiation
  • Contracts
  • Purchasing
  • Sales
  • Sponsorship
  • Conflict resolution

Learning Outcomes

  • apply business concepts to a realistic organizational problem
  • use evidence rather than preference
  • make and defend a decision under constraints
  • communicate in a professional role
  • respond to questions and revise thinking
  • explain individual contribution and learning

Teacher Preparation

  1. Select a challenge with a real decision, multiple defensible options, and clear constraints.
  2. Prepare a short public brief and any role-specific or confidential information.
  3. Define the required product, audience, time limit, and decision criteria.
  4. Decide which evidence students receive at the start and which evidence may be released later.
  5. Prepare a visible timer, checkpoint prompts, and an individual accountability task.

Room Setup

Arrange desks in team pods with one shared workspace per team. Create a separate decision area for presentations, interviews, negotiations, or executive questioning. Keep role packets face down until the briefing is complete.

Timing Options

45-Minute Sprint

Briefing 5, preparation 8, core simulation 17, decision or presentation 10, reflection 5.

90-Minute Block

Briefing 10, role preparation 12, analysis 20, simulation 25, questioning 15, revision and reflection 8.

Multi-Day Version

Day 1 evidence and planning, Day 2 simulation and decision, Day 3 revision, extension, and assessment.

Teacher Language

  • What is your underlying interest, not just your stated position?
  • What can you trade that costs you less than it is worth to them?
  • What is your best alternative if no agreement is reached?
  • Are you making concessions or exchanging value?
  • Is the final agreement specific enough to implement?

Course Examples

Marketing

Negotiate an influencer, media, sponsorship, or agency agreement.

Personal Finance

Negotiate salary, rent, a vehicle purchase, or repayment terms.

Entrepreneurship

Negotiate with a supplier, investor, landlord, or distribution partner.

Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Negotiate naming rights, athlete endorsement, venue use, or event sponsorship.

AP Business

Students identify interests, alternatives, leverage, value creation, relationship risk, and implementation terms.

Common Problems and Fixes

Students argue positions instead of exploring interests

Require an interest map and three diagnostic questions before offers.

The negotiation becomes only about price

Include several issues with different priorities.

Students agree too quickly

Require two packages and a written comparison before signing.

Agreements stay vague

Use a final contract summary with exact responsibilities, deadlines, and consequences.

Variations

One-on-One Deal

Use pairs and a short confidential brief for quick practice.

Team Negotiation

Use functional roles on each side when the deal has financial, operational, and relationship issues.

Multi-Party Negotiation

Add a third stakeholder with veto power or unique resources. Use this for advanced classes.

Deadline Pressure

Introduce a late deadline or competing offer. Use this to explore urgency and leverage.

Renegotiation

Reveal that one condition changed after the agreement. Use this to practice relationship repair and contract adaptation.

Assessment

Score the quality of the decision, not the performance alone.

Student Reflection

  • What evidence most influenced your team's decision?
  • What tradeoff was hardest to manage?
  • What did another role notice that you initially missed?
  • What would you revise after hearing questions or feedback?
  • What would a real organization need before implementing this decision?
