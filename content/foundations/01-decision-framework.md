# The Business Teacher's Decision Framework

## You've Probably Been Here

The bell rings in three minutes.

Half the class was absent yesterday because of a field trip. The internet is crawling. Your original lesson is not going to work.

Now what?

Experienced business teachers do not start with, “Which activity should I use?” They start with, “What should students be able to do by the end of class?”

## Start With the Objective, Not the Activity

Gallery Walks can be useful. Shark Tank pitches can be memorable. Debates can be excellent.

None of those matter if they do not move students toward the day's objective.

The strategy serves the lesson - not the other way around.

## The Four Questions

1. What should students know or be able to do by the end of class?
2. Do they need to think, practice, discuss, create, or decide?
3. What will probably get in the way?
4. What is the backup if Plan A falls apart?

## Business Example

Suppose the topic is why Costco and Sam's Club charge membership fees.

A short explanation communicates the basic model.

Think–Pair–Share helps students compare the value proposition.

Four Corners forces them to defend whether the membership is worth it.

A case discussion asks what the company should do if renewals begin falling.

Same topic. Different thinking. Different strategy.

## Five-Minute Fix

Running short on time does not mean abandoning the lesson.

Cut the number of scenarios. Reduce the number of rotations. Ask students to defend one answer instead of three.

Protect the thinking even when the activity gets smaller.
