# The Business Educator's Playbook

## You've Probably Been Here

It's second block on a Thursday.

You planned a lesson on pricing strategies. The opener was solid. The slides look good. Then you ask your first question.

“Why do you think Starbucks can charge more than the gas station down the road?”

Silence.

One student finally says, “Because it's Starbucks.”

Another shrugs.

Now everyone is looking at you to carry the conversation.

If you've taught business for any length of time, you've lived this moment. The problem usually isn't the topic. Students often haven't been given enough reason—or enough time—to think before they're asked to talk.

That moment is why this playbook exists.

## Why Business Class Is Different

Business class asks students to make decisions, defend ideas, solve messy problems, evaluate risk, communicate professionally, and think like owners, managers, marketers, investors, employees, and consumers.

That requires a different toolbox.

This is not a collection of generic educational activities with business vocabulary pasted on top. Every strategy begins with an actual classroom problem and connects to realistic business decisions.

## How to Use This Site

Use this as a reference, not a book you must read in order.

Start with the problem you are trying to solve:

- Students will not talk.
- The class needs movement.
- Discussion feels shallow.
- Groups finish too early.
- You need a stronger case-study lesson.
- You lost technology.
- You only have twenty minutes.

The strategy pages show what to use, why it works, what usually goes wrong, and how to adjust it.
