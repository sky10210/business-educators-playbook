# Escape Room

## Quick Scan

**One-line summary:** Organize review around connected puzzles that require content knowledge and collaboration.

**Typical time:** 40–90 minutes  
**Technology:** Optional  
**45-minute fit:** Limited  
**90-minute fit:** Excellent  
**Best teacher entry point:** Experienced Facilitator

## Why You'll Keep Using It

Organize review around connected puzzles that require content knowledge and collaboration. It creates a repeatable structure students can learn quickly, which means less time explaining the activity and more time using business evidence.

## Best Used When

Use this strategy when:
- Need cumulative review
- Students need teamwork
- Review feels repetitive

## Before Class

Choose one clear business decision. Prepare only the evidence students truly need. Decide what every student must produce so participation can be seen rather than assumed.

## How I Run It

1. Frame the business decision.
2. Give students a concrete product: a ranking, recommendation, calculation, question, or defense.
3. Set a visible time limit.
4. Circulate and ask for evidence rather than giving answers.
5. End with a decision, not merely a summary.

## 45-Minute Example

- **0–5:** Business hook and individual prediction
- **5–10:** Explain the task and success criteria
- **10–28:** Run Escape Room
- **28–38:** Compare decisions and evidence
- **38–43:** Whole-class debrief
- **43–45:** Exit ticket

## 90-Minute Example

- **0–10:** Hook, retrieval, and context
- **10–20:** Model one example
- **20–55:** Main Escape Room structure
- **55–70:** Revision using peer or teacher feedback
- **70–83:** Present or defend recommendations
- **83–88:** Connect to course concepts
- **88–90:** Reflection

## Teacher Script

> “Your goal is not to finish first. Your goal is to make the strongest business decision you can defend.”

> “Show me the evidence that changed your mind.”

> “What risk is your recommendation creating?”

## Student Directions

1. Read the business situation.
2. Identify the decision that must be made.
3. Use the assigned structure.
4. Record evidence.
5. Make one final recommendation.
6. Explain one risk or limitation.

## Business Example

Solve connected personal-finance clues to unlock a final financial recommendation.

## Tiny Tweaks

- Reduce the number of choices for freshmen.
- Add numerical evidence for advanced students.
- Require a stakeholder perspective in AP Business.
- Run the final reflection silently when the room is overstimulated.
- Replace a full-class share with one written recommendation per group.

## Common Mistakes

The strategy weakens when the prompt has an obvious answer, the task is too broad, or group participation is invisible. Avoid adding more directions when the real issue is unclear decision criteria.

## Classroom Decision Guide

**Choose this strategy when:** the class needs review and a visible decision.

**Use something simpler when:** students lack the basic content knowledge needed to participate.

**Use something deeper when:** the lesson requires extended research, financial modeling, or a formal client deliverable.

## Differentiation

**Support:** sentence starters, role cards, reduced choices, pre-highlighted evidence.  
**Extension:** conflicting data, weighted criteria, stakeholder constraints, or a required counterargument.

## If I Were Teaching AP Business

Require students to identify the issue, cite evidence, name the decision criteria, evaluate alternatives, and explain the risk of the final recommendation.

## If This Were Freshmen

Use one familiar company, three choices or fewer, a visible timer, and a model response. Grade the reasoning before the vocabulary.
