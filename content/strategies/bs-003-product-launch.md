# Product Launch

## Quick Scan

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| Time | 60–180 minutes |
| Prep | High |
| Movement | Moderate |
| Collaboration | Very High |
| Technology | Optional |
| Best Group Size | 4–6 students |

## Best Uses

- Marketing strategy
- Entrepreneurship
- Cross-functional decision making
- Product development
- Pricing and distribution
- Consumer research
- Sports and entertainment marketing
- AP Business

## Why I Use It

Product Launch helps students understand that successful products do not move directly from a creative idea to an advertisement. A launch requires coordinated decisions across research, product design, finance, operations, marketing, distribution, and customer experience.

Students work as a cross-functional launch team responsible for bringing a product or service to market. They must identify a target customer, define the problem being solved, build a value proposition, make realistic pricing and distribution decisions, develop promotional communication, anticipate operational risks, and defend the entire plan as one connected strategy.

The strength of the simulation is integration. Students quickly see that an exciting promotion cannot rescue a weak product, a low price may undermine profitability, an ambitious distribution plan may exceed capacity, and a good idea may fail if the customer does not understand its value.

## Learning Outcomes

Students will be able to:

- define a target market using relevant evidence,
- identify a customer problem or unmet need,
- develop a clear value proposition,
- connect product features to customer benefits,
- establish launch objectives and success measures,
- evaluate pricing alternatives,
- select realistic distribution channels,
- create an integrated promotional plan,
- estimate launch costs and financial implications,
- identify operational and reputational risks,
- coordinate decisions across business functions, and
- present and defend a complete launch recommendation.

## Before Class

### Choose the Launch Challenge

Decide whether all teams will launch the same product, choose from a teacher-approved list, or develop an original concept.

A strong challenge should include:

- a recognizable customer problem,
- enough flexibility for different strategies,
- realistic constraints,
- a defined launch market,
- a budget or resource limit,
- meaningful pricing and channel choices, and
- a clear final deliverable.

Possible launch challenges include:

- a hydration product for student athletes,
- a budgeting app for first-time workers,
- a new minor-league fan experience,
- a sustainable school-supply line,
- a subscription service for local businesses,
- a new restaurant concept,
- an event or music-festival package,
- a school-based enterprise product,
- a travel service for young adults, or
- a new feature for an existing brand.

### Establish Constraints

Provide enough constraints to force decisions. Useful constraints include:

- fixed launch budget,
- limited production capacity,
- required profit margin,
- restricted launch geography,
- deadline,
- target age group,
- legal or safety requirements,
- approved sales channels,
- competitor information, and
- expected sales target.

### Prepare the Launch Packet

A complete packet may include:

- launch brief,
- customer profile,
- market data,
- competitor snapshots,
- cost information,
- channel options,
- promotional rate card,
- decision worksheets,
- launch-plan template,
- presentation rubric, and
- individual reflection.

## Room Setup

Seat students in cross-functional launch teams. Each team should have one central workspace and access to all shared exhibits.

For longer versions, create optional functional stations:

- Research and Customer Insights
- Product and Operations
- Finance and Pricing
- Marketing Communications
- Distribution and Sales

Teams may visit stations to gather information, complete calculations, or receive teacher approval before advancing.

# Running the Strategy

## Phase 1: Launch Briefing

1. Introduce the product category, market, and challenge.
2. Explain the launch deadline, budget, and required deliverables.
3. Review the evaluation criteria.
4. Clarify which information is fixed and which decisions belong to the team.
5. Assign or allow teams to select functional roles.

### Teacher Language

- Your job is not simply to invent a product. Your job is to build a launch that can work.
- Every major decision must connect to the target customer.
- A strong launch is one strategy, not a collection of disconnected ideas.
- You will need to explain what you chose, what you rejected, and why.

## Phase 2: Customer and Market Analysis

Teams begin by defining whom the launch is for and what problem the product solves.

Require teams to identify:

- primary target market,
- secondary target market if appropriate,
- customer need or frustration,
- purchase motivation,
- likely objection,
- current alternatives,
- relevant trend or market evidence, and
- one insight that should shape the launch.

### Checkpoint

Before teams develop branding or promotion, require approval of a concise customer problem statement:

> Our target customer is ___, who needs ___ because ___. Existing options fail because ___.

## Phase 3: Product and Value Proposition

Teams define the product, service, or experience.

They should establish:

- core product,
- key features,
- customer benefits,
- point of difference,
- brand promise,
- proof or reason to believe,
- product limitations, and
- minimum viable launch version.

### Feature–Benefit Test

For every major feature, students should answer:

1. What is it?
2. What does it do?
3. Why should the target customer care?

### Teacher Language

- Is that a feature or a benefit?
- What customer problem does this solve?
- Which feature is essential, and which is merely interesting?
- What makes this meaningfully different from the closest alternative?

## Phase 4: Pricing and Financial Viability

Teams select a pricing approach and test whether it supports the overall launch.

Possible approaches:

- cost-plus pricing,
- competitor-based pricing,
- value-based pricing,
- penetration pricing,
- premium pricing,
- freemium,
- subscription pricing,
- bundle pricing, or
- dynamic pricing.

Require students to address:

- unit or service cost,
- proposed price,
- contribution margin,
- expected sales volume,
- launch expenses,
- break-even point when appropriate,
- discount strategy,
- financial risk, and
- how price affects positioning.

### Teacher Language

- What does this price communicate about the product?
- Can your target customer afford it?
- Can the business sustain it?
- How many units must be sold before the launch pays for itself?
- What happens if demand is lower than expected?

## Phase 5: Distribution and Customer Access

Teams decide where and how customers will obtain the product.

Possible channels include:

- direct-to-consumer website,
- mobile app,
- school store,
- retail partner,
- marketplace platform,
- event sales,
- subscription delivery,
- wholesale distribution,
- social commerce, or
- business-to-business sales.

Require teams to consider:

- customer convenience,
- channel cost,
- control over the customer experience,
- inventory or capacity,
- geographic reach,
- delivery speed,
- partner requirements, and
- channel conflict.

## Phase 6: Integrated Promotion

Teams create a promotional plan designed to move the target customer from awareness to action.

The plan should identify:

- launch message,
- central customer benefit,
- communication tone,
- promotional channels,
- call to action,
- launch event or activation,
- budget allocation,
- timing, and
- success measures.

Possible channels:

- social media,
- email,
- digital advertising,
- influencer partnerships,
- sponsorships,
- public relations,
- direct marketing,
- sales promotion,
- events,
- in-store promotion, and
- personal selling.

### Integration Check

Ask teams to explain how product, price, place, and promotion reinforce the same position.

## Phase 7: Operations and Launch Readiness

Students identify what must happen behind the scenes before the launch can succeed.

Require teams to address:

- suppliers,
- production or service capacity,
- staffing,
- training,
- inventory,
- technology,
- quality control,
- customer support,
- returns or complaints,
- legal and ethical concerns, and
- launch-day responsibilities.

### Readiness Question

> What could create strong customer demand but still cause the launch to fail operationally?

## Phase 8: Risk Register and Contingency Plan

Teams identify at least four launch risks.

For each risk, record:

- likelihood,
- potential impact,
- early warning sign,
- prevention strategy,
- contingency response, and
- responsible team member.

Common risks include:

- weak demand,
- negative reviews,
- supplier delays,
- production defects,
- budget overruns,
- competitor response,
- confusing messaging,
- pricing resistance,
- legal concerns,
- technology failure, and
- insufficient staffing.

## Phase 9: Launch Plan and Timeline

Teams turn their decisions into an action sequence.

A strong timeline includes:

- prelaunch research,
- product preparation,
- pricing approval,
- channel setup,
- promotional production,
- employee or partner training,
- launch day,
- first-week review,
- 30-day review, and
- revision decision.

Every action should have an owner, deadline, and success indicator.

## Phase 10: Executive Launch Pitch

Teams present the launch as if requesting final approval from senior leadership or investors.

A recommended pitch structure:

1. Customer problem
2. Target market insight
3. Product and value proposition
4. Competitive advantage
5. Pricing strategy
6. Distribution plan
7. Promotional campaign
8. Financial outlook
9. Launch timeline
10. Risks and contingencies
11. Approval request

## Phase 11: Questions, Decision, and Revision

After each pitch, the audience asks cross-functional questions. Teams should not divide questions by role; every member must understand the complete launch.

Require teams to revise one decision after receiving feedback. This keeps the activity focused on learning rather than performance alone.

# Launch Team Roles

## Launch Director

- coordinates the complete launch,
- manages deadlines,
- resolves cross-functional conflicts,
- checks alignment, and
- leads the final approval request.

## Customer Insights Lead

- defines the target market,
- interprets research,
- identifies customer needs,
- evaluates competitors, and
- ensures decisions remain customer-centered.

## Product and Operations Lead

- defines features and quality standards,
- evaluates feasibility,
- plans production or service delivery,
- manages capacity concerns, and
- develops launch-readiness requirements.

## Finance and Pricing Lead

- estimates costs,
- evaluates pricing options,
- calculates margins or break-even,
- monitors the launch budget, and
- explains financial risks.

## Marketing Communications Lead

- develops positioning and messaging,
- selects promotional channels,
- creates the launch campaign,
- allocates promotional spending, and
- defines communication measures.

## Distribution and Sales Lead

- selects sales channels,
- evaluates customer access,
- plans partnerships,
- identifies channel costs, and
- defines the sales process.

For smaller teams, combine roles. For larger teams, add Brand Manager, Data Analyst, Public Relations Lead, or Customer Experience Lead.

# Teacher Language Library

## Customer Focus

- Who is the first customer most likely to buy this?
- What problem is urgent enough to motivate action?
- Which evidence supports your target-market decision?
- What objection will the customer have?

## Product Strategy

- Which feature creates the greatest customer value?
- What is your point of difference?
- Could a competitor copy this easily?
- What have you intentionally left out of the first launch?

## Finance

- Which assumption has the greatest effect on your forecast?
- Does the budget match the strategy?
- What happens to profit if costs rise?
- Are you buying awareness or generating demand?

## Promotion

- What is the one message customers should remember?
- Why is this channel appropriate for this audience?
- What action should the customer take next?
- How will you know whether promotion worked?

## Operations

- Can the organization deliver what marketing promises?
- Where is the capacity bottleneck?
- What must be tested before launch day?
- Who handles a dissatisfied first customer?

## Strategic Alignment

- Which decision does not fit the rest of your plan?
- What tradeoff did the team make?
- What would you change with twice the budget?
- What would you protect if the budget were cut by 25 percent?

# Course Examples

## Business Foundations

Students launch a new product for the school store. Require a target customer, feature–benefit chart, price, sales location, promotional message, budget, and first-month goal.

## Marketing

Teams launch a beverage for high-school athletes. They use segment data, develop positioning, compare competitors, select packaging, choose a price, build a channel plan, and create an integrated promotional campaign.

## Personal Finance

Teams launch a financial tool for first-time earners. Possible products include a budgeting app, savings challenge, prepaid debit feature, or financial coaching subscription. Require attention to fees, trust, privacy, accessibility, and consumer protection.

## Entrepreneurship

Students launch a minimum viable product for a startup. Require customer validation, cost estimates, revenue model, channel choice, launch assumptions, and a plan for using early feedback.

## Economics

Teams launch a product in a market affected by inflation, changing demand, or supply constraints. Students explain how market conditions influence price, production, and launch timing.

## Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Teams launch a fan-experience product, alternate jersey, membership package, esports event, music festival feature, or sponsor activation. Require audience fit, event operations, pricing, partnerships, promotion, and measurable fan response.

## AP Business

Teams respond to a detailed launch case with cross-functional evidence. Require quantitative analysis, alternatives, stakeholder impact, operational feasibility, limitations, risk, implementation, and a justified recommendation.

# Common Problems and Fixes

## Students Start With a Logo

Do not allow branding work until the target customer, problem statement, and value proposition are approved.

## The Product Has Too Many Features

Require teams to identify the minimum viable launch and justify each feature through a customer benefit.

## Promotion Is Just Social Media

Require at least three communication methods with different functions in the customer journey.

## Pricing Is a Guess

Require students to compare cost, competitor, customer-value, and positioning considerations.

## The Budget Does Not Match the Plan

Have teams label every tactic with a cost and remove anything that exceeds the budget.

## Teams Ignore Distribution

Require students to describe exactly how the first customer discovers, purchases, receives, and uses the product.

## Functional Roles Work Separately

Use alignment checkpoints where each role must explain how its decision affects two other functions.

## Presentations Become Design Contests

Score evidence, integration, feasibility, and decision quality more heavily than visual polish.

## Students Forecast Unrealistic Sales

Require a stated assumption, comparable evidence, and conservative scenario.

# Variations

## Product Launch War Room

Release a major problem shortly before launch, such as a supplier delay, negative review, competitor announcement, or quality issue.

## Relaunch

Teams repair and relaunch an unsuccessful existing product.

## Limited-Budget Launch

All teams receive the same restricted budget and must make visible tradeoffs.

## International Launch

Teams adapt the product, message, price, and channel strategy for another country.

## Seasonal Launch

Students plan around a fixed selling window such as back-to-school, holidays, playoffs, or summer travel.

## Service Launch

Launch a service, subscription, event, or experience rather than a physical product.

## Competing Launch Teams

Multiple teams work for the same company and compete for executive approval.

## Progressive Market Update

Release new customer, competitor, cost, or demand information during the simulation.

## Live Client Launch

A local business or school organization provides the challenge and feedback.

## Strong Launch Indicators

A strong launch plan is:

- customer-centered,
- differentiated,
- financially realistic,
- operationally feasible,
- integrated across functions,
- specific about execution,
- measurable,
- honest about risk, and
- adaptable when evidence changes.

- role-specific work log,
- individual launch recommendation,
- response to an executive question,
- peer contribution rating,
- post-launch reflection,
- revision explanation, or
- short calculation completed independently.

# Final Thoughts

Product Launch is most effective when students experience the tension between creativity and execution. The activity should reward an integrated, defensible plan rather than the flashiest product or presentation.

The teacher's role is to keep teams focused on alignment. Each decision should reinforce the same customer, value proposition, and launch objective. When students must connect research, finance, operations, distribution, and communication, they begin to understand how real business functions depend on one another.

Used repeatedly, the framework can become a familiar capstone structure. Students learn to approach any launch by asking who the customer is, what value is being created, how the organization will deliver it, what the economics require, what could go wrong, and how success will be measured.
