# Shark Tank

## Quick Scan

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| Time | 30–90 minutes |
| Prep | Moderate |
| Movement | Moderate |
| Collaboration | Very High |
| Technology | Optional |

## Best Uses

- Entrepreneurship pitches
- Marketing campaign proposals
- New-product development
- Financial decision making
- AP Business recommendations
- Project-based learning

## Why I Use It

Shark Tank gives students an authentic reason to combine research, financial thinking, marketing, operations, and communication. Students must do more than present an idea: they must explain why it creates value, defend their assumptions, answer questions, and revise based on feedback.

The strongest classroom version is not a television-show imitation. It is a structured decision-making simulation in which students act as founders, managers, investors, or evaluators and use business evidence to support a recommendation.

## Before Class

Decide what students are pitching:

- a startup,
- a new product,
- a marketing campaign,
- an event concept,
- a business improvement,
- an investment proposal, or
- a strategic recommendation.

Provide clear constraints such as:

- target customer,
- available budget,
- required financial information,
- presentation length,
- evidence expectations, and
- scoring criteria.

## Materials

- Pitch planning guide
- Evaluation rubric
- Financial assumptions sheet
- Timer
- Optional presentation slides
- Investor feedback form
- Reflection or revision sheet

## Room Setup

Create a clear presentation area. Evaluators should sit where they can see the presenters and any visuals. The remaining students need an active role such as scoring, questioning, comparing, or identifying evidence.

# Running the Strategy

1. Introduce the challenge, audience, and decision criteria.
2. Give teams time to develop their proposal.
3. Require every team to identify the customer problem, proposed solution, value proposition, and evidence.
4. Have teams prepare a concise pitch.
5. Assign investor or evaluator roles.
6. Run timed presentations.
7. Allow a structured question-and-answer period.
8. Require evaluators to make and justify a decision.
9. Give teams feedback.
10. End with revision, reflection, or a second-round pitch.

## Core Pitch Structure

A strong student pitch should answer:

1. What problem exists?
2. Who experiences the problem?
3. What is the proposed solution?
4. Why is this solution different or valuable?
5. How will the organization reach customers?
6. What will it cost?
7. How will it generate revenue or measurable value?
8. What evidence supports the proposal?
9. What are the major risks?
10. What decision or investment is being requested?

## Teacher Language

- Lead with the problem, not the product.
- State the assumption behind your recommendation.
- Separate evidence from opinion.
- Explain how the idea creates value for the customer and the organization.
- A strong answer may acknowledge a weakness and explain how the team would manage it.
- Investors should challenge the proposal, not the presenters.
- Feedback must identify both a strength and a next step.

## Timing Options

### 30-Minute Mini Pitch

- Challenge and planning: 10 minutes
- Four 3-minute pitches: 12 minutes
- Questions and decisions: 6 minutes
- Reflection: 2 minutes

### 55-Minute Class

- Introduction: 5 minutes
- Team planning: 15 minutes
- Pitches and questions: 25 minutes
- Investor decisions: 5 minutes
- Reflection: 5 minutes

### 90-Minute Block

- Challenge and criteria: 10 minutes
- Research and planning: 25 minutes
- Pitch preparation: 10 minutes
- Presentations and questions: 30 minutes
- Investment decisions: 5 minutes
- Revision and reflection: 10 minutes

## Course Examples

### Business Foundations

Students pitch a small business that could operate successfully in their community. They must identify the customer, need, startup costs, competition, and one major risk.

### Marketing

Teams pitch a campaign for an existing product. The pitch must include the target market, positioning, promotional mix, media choices, estimated budget, and success metrics.

### Personal Finance

Students pitch a financial plan for a fictional client. They must defend priorities involving saving, debt, insurance, budgeting, or investing.

### Entrepreneurship

Teams pitch an original venture using a simplified business model. Require a customer problem, value proposition, revenue model, startup needs, competitive advantage, and funding request.

### Economics

Students pitch a policy or business response to an economic problem and explain expected benefits, costs, incentives, and unintended consequences.

### Sports & Entertainment Marketing

Teams pitch a new promotional event, sponsorship package, fan experience, or revenue strategy for a team, venue, artist, or festival.

### AP Business

Students pitch a strategic recommendation based on a case. Require evidence from multiple business functions, quantitative support where appropriate, stakeholder implications, risks, and a justified final recommendation.

## Investor and Evaluator Roles

Give evaluators defined responsibilities.

### Financial Investor

Focuses on:
- costs,
- revenue,
- profitability,
- cash needs,
- financial risk, and
- assumptions.

### Marketing Investor

Focuses on:
- target market,
- customer need,
- differentiation,
- promotion,
- pricing, and
- brand fit.

### Operations Investor

Focuses on:
- capacity,
- staffing,
- supply chain,
- quality,
- timelines, and
- feasibility.

### Stakeholder Investor

Focuses on:
- employees,
- customers,
- owners,
- community,
- ethics, and
- long-term consequences.

### Evidence Auditor

Identifies which claims are supported and which rely on assumptions.

## Freshman Supports

- Provide a one-page pitch organizer.
- Limit the pitch to four required sections.
- Give students sentence starters.
- Model a weak and strong pitch.
- Allow note cards.
- Assign group roles.
- Use a small fictional budget.
- Grade reasoning more heavily than presentation polish.

Helpful sentence starters:

- The customer problem is...
- Our solution creates value by...
- We believe customers will respond because...
- The most important cost is...
- Our biggest risk is...
- We would measure success by...

## AP-Level Expectations

Require students to:

- use case evidence,
- distinguish facts from assumptions,
- include quantitative analysis,
- connect multiple business functions,
- evaluate stakeholder impact,
- identify limitations,
- compare alternatives,
- justify the recommendation, and
- explain what evidence could change the decision.

## Common Problems and Fixes

### Presentations Become Style Over Substance

Use a rubric that gives more weight to evidence, feasibility, financial logic, and decision quality than visual design.

### Evaluators Ask Random Questions

Assign evaluator lenses and provide question stems.

### Students Invent Unrealistic Financial Numbers

Require assumptions to be listed and explained. Provide benchmark ranges when appropriate.

### One Student Delivers the Entire Pitch

Assign required speaking sections or allow evaluators to direct questions to specific team members.

### The Activity Takes Too Long

Use one-minute pitches, simultaneous investor panels, preliminary rounds, or recorded pitches.

### Feedback Becomes Personal

Require evaluators to address the proposal, evidence, and assumptions rather than the student.

### Students Think Every Idea Deserves Funding

Set an investment limit or require evaluators to rank proposals using the rubric.

## Variations

### One-Minute Elevator Pitch

Students present only the problem, solution, customer, and request.

### Reverse Shark Tank

Students act as investors who must persuade a business to accept their partnership offer.

### Social Enterprise Pitch

Teams must balance financial sustainability with measurable social impact.

### Internal Innovation Pitch

Students propose an improvement inside an existing company rather than creating a startup.

### Marketing Agency Pitch

Teams compete to win a fictional client by presenting a campaign recommendation.

### Budget Shark Tank

Every proposal must fit within a strict spending limit.

### Revision Round

Teams receive feedback, revise one weakness, and deliver a second pitch.

### Silent Shark Tank

Students submit one-page proposals that evaluators review and score without oral presentations.

## Assessment

A balanced rubric can include:

- Problem and customer understanding
- Value proposition
- Evidence and research
- Marketing logic
- Financial reasoning
- Operational feasibility
- Risk analysis
- Quality of recommendation
- Responses to questions
- Team participation
- Reflection and revision

### Suggested Weighting

| Area | Weight |
|---|---:|
| Business reasoning and evidence | 30% |
| Feasibility and financial logic | 20% |
| Marketing and customer fit | 15% |
| Risk and stakeholder analysis | 15% |
| Communication and responses | 10% |
| Reflection and revision | 10% |

## Final Thoughts

Shark Tank is most valuable when students must defend a decision rather than simply perform a presentation. Clear constraints, defined evaluator roles, evidence requirements, and a revision step turn the activity into a rigorous business simulation. Students learn that a persuasive idea depends not only on confidence, but also on customer value, financial logic, operational feasibility, and the ability to respond thoughtfully when assumptions are challenged.
